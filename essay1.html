The Semantics of Syntax Acquisition

By Sachith Mendis

How linked are syntax mastery and semantic understanding in the language acquisition process? Historically, American structural linguists—in their exploration of the acquisition of language—opted to focus on grammatical constructions, delegating the issue of semantics and communicative meaning to psychologists. Even as Chomsky upturned this linguistic paradigm in the 20th Century—dubbing linguistics a branch of cognitive psychology—his ideas on language innateness did not move away from this separation: in the nativist view, the issue of constructing grammar is unrelated to semantics and expression of meaning.

This essay will first present the nativist case for some innate human faculty for the acquisition of language—of which Chomskyan universal grammar is the extreme (Chomsky 2000). This stance rests on a ‘poverty of the stimulus’ (POS) argument that claims there is a large discrepancy between the input and output of the language acquisition process. This essay will flesh this out by treating empirical examples that demonstrate this discrepancy.

Many have criticised positing an innate language faculty, however, on various grounds (notably: Scholz and Pullum; Cowie 1998) including on the huge variation between grammars (Da̧browska). This paper will explore Tomasello’s critique of innateness, and his proposal that instead, children learn language through usage (2003, 2005, 2008). In this view, a child’s semantic understanding is intimately tied into how she constructs her rules for syntax, because she constructs syntax necessarily by using words to communicate about real intentions. This essay will show how Tomasello’s account criticises the nativist’s argument on their understanding of both the input and output of the language acquisition process.

As such, this paper will present a survey of both stances, and will posit the feasibility of a usage-based account with respect to supplementary evidence in favour of the nativist stance. Here it shows that the core claim of the nativist argument is accounted for in the constructionist frame, but also that there is further empirical work to be done to robustly explain some counter-intuitive observations. It does this to elucidate these two sides of the language acquisition debate, and to explore what the role of semantics in syntax mastery might be.

Poverty of the Stimulus

A foundational piece—perhaps the foundational piece—of evidence in favour of language innateness is the ‘poverty of the stimulus’ (POS) argument which claims that a learner’s input when trying to learn language—the utterances of people around them, or the primary linguistic data (PLD)—would be inadequate to produce in them an output of a mastery of language at a level of sophistication that most people reach after childhood. Based on this impoverished input, nativists—like Chomsky—posit an innate ‘language acquisition device’ which accounts for the language learner’s mastery of language. This innate faculty for learning language is domain-specific—its inputs (the PLD) are only processed through this ‘module’, and this ‘module’ does not process any other sort of input —and has been theorised to function (non-exhaustively) through principles and parameters (Baker; J. D. Fodor and Sakas), and Merge recursion (Berwick et al.; Chomsky 2004, 2012).

Formally, the argument can be articulated as (Laurence and Margolis 221):

  1. In the PLD of a learner of language, there are numerous ‘grammars’ which are consistent with the target language. The correct grammar the learner is trying to learn isn’t the only syntactic set of rules that is consistent with the linguistic input she has.
  2. The correct grammar isn’t necessarily the simplest, or most intuitive of this set of consistent grammars. In this way, an “empiricist learner” would not be intuitively drawn to selecting the correct grammar out of the set of consistent grammars.
  3. The PLD does not consist of the sort of data that would allow the learner to correctly choose the appropriate grammar. In addition to the learner not hearing enough sentences (especially those that demonstrate exceptions in their target grammar), the PLD is of a fundamentally different type to what is required.
  4. If children were empiricist learners, propositions 1–3 show that they would not be able to “reliably arrive at the correct grammar for their language.”
  5. Despite this, children do learn to choose the correct grammar.
  6. Children are therefore not “empiricist learners”: there would not be enough information in their linguistic input (the PLD) for them to acquire a mastery of their relevant grammar to the level that is typical.

In this way, the knowledge required to master language, or the knowledge acquired by mastering language, is not accounted for the by the PLD. There is simply not enough information—there is a poverty of stimulus—in a learner’s linguistic input for them to end up with as sophisticated a mastery of language as is typical. Because of this, learners can’t be “empiricist” (Laurence and Margolis 221) in their method of language acquisition.

Laurence and Margolis describe their empiricist learner as “one that instantiates an empiricist learning theory. Among other things, such a learner wouldn’t have any innate domain-specific knowledge or biases to guide her learning and, in particular, wouldn’t have any innate language-specific knowledge or biases” (221; my emphasis). If the discrepancy between the information in a learner’s PLD and in their mastery of language is as large as Laurence and Margolis claim, then a learner can’t use an “empiricist learning theory” (of observation and inference grounded on repetition) to master language. Laurence and Margolis take this as evidence that there must be some sort of “domain-specific … [or] language-specific knowledge” (221) in humans, as children do in fact master language.

This conclusion rests heavily on the discrepancy between the information in a learner’s PLD, and in their mastery of language. As such, Laurence and Margolis’ treatment of the poverty of the stimulus argument largely rests on examples that demonstrate this discrepancy.

A central example used in the nativist argument is the construction of a question from a sentence with two (or more) auxiliary verbs.

  1. The car is red.
  2. Is the car red?
  3. The park is wet because it rained.
  4. Is the park wet because it rained?

From the above, it seems like the rule behind converting a sentence (1, 3) to a question (2, 4) is to move the first ‘is’ to the beginning of the sentence. This rule, however, fails when we add another ‘is’ (a second auxiliary verb) to the sentence:

  1. The car that is red is broken.
  2. * Is the car that red is broken?

In this sentence—whose noun phrase contains the auxiliary verb ‘is’ (the noun phrase in (5) is “the car that is red”)—we are supposed to ignore the first ‘is’ and instead move the auxiliary verb of the main clause to the beginning to form the correct sentence:

  1. Is the car that is red broken?

The construction of this sentence, argue Laurence and Margolis, requires a nuanced understanding of what the main clause of a sentence is, and what differentiates auxiliary verbs from lexical verbs. They argue—in line with Chomsky (1980, 40)—that young learners would not be exposed to enough example sentences to distinguish between auxiliary and lexical verbs in this way, and as such wouldn’t be able to garner the correct rule to prefer sentence (7) over the more intuitive (6): there is a quantitative deficiency in a learner’s input.

Laurence and Margolis cite the studies of Karin Stromswold (1990, 1999, 2000) as evidence that it is uncharacteristic for children to make mistakes like those which follow from an inability to distinguish between lexical and auxiliary verbs. This is evidence that a child’s mastery of language is, at least through the lens of formal grammar, in this example quite sophisticated.

To finish, some other examples of suggested discrepancies between the PLD and formal language are: Differentiating between ‘linguistic’ sounds (like syllables, words etc.) and other human-made sounds like coughs, whistles, or sneezes; Recognising common units of useful linguistic sounds which are expressed differently, like the same word being spoken with slurred speech, an accent, whispered, or shouted; The same phonological element of a language having different phonetic realisations (like the ‘p’ in ‘pit’ and ‘spit’) and different phonological elements have the same phonetic realisation (like ‘threw’ and ‘through’). Even if the specifics of how these are accounted for in a nativist account of language acquisition are far from straightforward, these difficulties in translating an input of purely phonetical linguistic data into a sophisticated understanding of grammar are important questions to be posed to the non-nativist.

Semantics

Michael Tomasello (Ibbotson and Tomasello; Tomasello 2003, Tomasello 2005, Tomasello 2008) counters this view. He does this on two fronts: first by claiming the input a learner receives is not as impoverished as nativists claim, and second by arguing that nativists misunderstand grammar as only its formal rules, and hence exaggerate how complicated it is for children to have mastered language. Tomasello proposes a usage-based theory of language acquisition instead, which attributes a child’s ability to learn language to the domain-general faculties of “intention-reading” and “pattern-finding” (2008, 69), in contrast to the domain-specific language acquisition device posited in the last section.

In this view, a child’s capacity to master syntactic rules is intimately linked to her approach to garnering semantic understanding. Children learn the grammatical structures of a language by using semantically loaded language to communicate their intentions to the people around them, and by understanding the communicative intentions of people talking to them: intention expression and its meaning underlie linguistic ability, and an account of linguistic comprehension should be predicated on an account of how people communicate in this way. This section will go over why semantics should be considered in a theory on syntax-learning in order to avoid the ‘linking problem’, and how a usage-based theory of language acquisition might work.

If a child has innate knowledge of various linguistic objects and structures, how are they able to apply this knowledge to the vocabulary they have learnt? Proponents of universal grammar (UG) have to explain how such a grammar would ‘link’ itself to individual different languages. Solutions such as the ‘principles and parameters’ approach have been suggested to solve this issue, but none have conclusively demonstrated to account for the immense variation we see in language across the world (Tomasello 2008).

In the nativist schema, even if a leaner were imbued with an innate sense of linguistic objects, they have no way of identifying what various linguistic objects pertain to which words. For example, how would a learner of Sinhalese, who has pre-existent knowledge of what verbs, noun phrases, and subjects are, be able to link those objects to a particular phonetical realisation, as in the sentence below?

  1. Wessa nisaa udyaanaya thethayi.

While nativists don’t claim that learners would necessarily understand (8), there must be some sense in which they are able to recognise individual linguistic objects within the sentence—like the noun phrase “udyaanaya” (park)—in order to apply their innate knowledge of grammatical rules. How does the nativist learner differentiate between the two clauses in the sentence “wessa nisaa” (because it rained) and “udyaanaya thethayi” (the park is wet), so as to correctly append the right clause (“wessa nisaa”) with “-de” to make this sentence a question (in a similar way to how we construct (4) from (3))?

An understanding of grammar as being constructed by “the functional dimension [of speech]” (Tomasello 2005, 184) does not fall prey to this ‘linking problem’. Because language serves the function of communicating intent, there is a semantic understanding of what is being talked about, prior to a mastery of grammar. Any understanding of syntactic rules is necessarily attached to the meaning of phrases, and what they communicate.

Tomasello’s (2008) usage-based theory argues grammatical structures arise from using words: linguistic structures are constructed by communicating meaning. We can see this hierarchy by observing how infants communicate. According to Tomasello, “human infants communicate in some fairly sophisticated ways before they have acquired any linguistic conventions” (2009, 70), for example by pointing and gesturing.

Without a ‘linking problem’ the information needed to master a language is not disproportionately complex. Children don’t learn formal grammatical structures and then link them to particular semantically-laden phrases—they use semantically-laden phrases to communicate intentions, and implicitly construct a rulebook for syntactic structure in the process. In this way, “people use linguistic conventions to achieve social ends”, and “meaning-based grammatical constructions emerge from individual acts of language use” (Tomasello 2008, 69). As such, mastering the complexity of formal grammar (which even baffles linguists) is not necessary to master language.

To acquire these “linguistic conventions,” children use their faculties of “intention-reading” and “pattern-finding” (Tomasello 2008, 69), which are domain-general facets of cognition (in contrast to the domain-specific language acquisition device posited by nativists). Children use their ability to read intentions to understand what speakers around them are trying to communicate. As such, while adults might be using linguistic means to communicate to a child who hasn’t mastered language, there is still communicative or intentional information that the child garners.

Because young learners are reading intentions when spoken to, the input of this language acquisition process isn’t as impoverished as argued in the previous section. There is non-linguistic information that learners receive when learning language: the intentions of the person speaking to/around them. With this in mind, the examples that constitute the POS argument aren’t as salient.

For example, in understanding how to construct (7), then, children would not make the mistake made in (6), because they would be referring to an object (a red car). They would have the concepts of ‘the red car’ and something being ‘broken,’ and would combine them as in (5). In constructing a question out of this, they would still have these two concepts, and the re-ordering of verbs—with each verb being associated to either ‘the red car’ or ‘broken’—would follow a pattern that has been constructed on the basis of similar concepts (as in (2)). In a sence, the child would have learnt to distinguish what, for example, the noun phrase of a sentence was, not because they formally understand its structure in syntactical terms, but rather because—by the time they start learning language—they would have become accustomed to communicating about real objects, like ‘the red car’.

Lastly, children use pattern finding—a skill they might use for other cognitive tasks, like pairing a long curved yellow object with the taste of a banana—to create “abstract linguistic schemas or constructions” from an input of “individual utterances” (Tomasello 2008, 70). It is by means of their pattern finding faculty that children are able to implicitly extrapolate individual instances of grammatical sentences into robust rules with which they can interpret and create completely novel sentences.

Supplementary Arguments

Laurence and Margolis further argue in favour of language innateness with a set of supplementary arguments that favour the nativist position. This section will deal with two sets of related arguments, and explore them from the perspective of a Tomasello style usage-based theory of language acquisition.

“All Normal Children Acquire Language” (Laurence and Margolis 234) and “Language Acquisition Is Independent of Intelligence” (237). This pertinent observation likely served as the impetus for the postulation of an innate faculty for the acquisition of language: it seems remarkable that all children (barring a miniscule minority) master language, seemingly independently of various contingencies, such as education. Spoken language is not taught, like other cognitive skills are (e.g. maths, playing the piano, or even reading). It is unique in that children are able to consistently acquire a mastery of spoken language, and the level of mastery between individuals with wildly different capacities in other cognitive tasks (e.g. maths etc.) is not proportionately different.

This is consistent with a usage-based account of language acquisition. All social children need to communicate their intentions, and are communicated to linguistically, and as such have the basis for constructing grammar through use. Other cognitive skills (e.g. proficiency in maths etc.) are not gathered through usage in the manner spoken language is: language is still a unique cognitive faculty in this view, as in the nativist view, and hence its unrelatedness to this sort of learned cognitive faculty is unsurprising.

We would expect, however, cognitive skills developed through the faculties of intention reading and pattern finding to exhibit a similar sort of ubiquity to language mastery, or at least to be more universal than the sort of learned skill mentioned above. On the face of it, this seems like a reasonable thing to expect to observe, but this is an empirical question that proponents would benefit from exploring.

“Language Acquisition Exhibits Ordered Developmental Stages” (Laurence and Margolis 235) and “Critical Period Effects” (236). The fact that the language acquisition process is very similar in all learners, again regardless of environment, is an argument in favour of the nativist. If children learned empirically, we would expect their progress to be contingent on what they’ve been exposed, what methods they’ve used to construct their grammar, and sheer luck. This is not what we observe: deaf children and blind children, for example, seem to reach language milestones at similar times to other children, despite “profound differences in learning environment” (235). Native signers consistently have a better lifelong grasp of grammar than early or late signers, and the cases of Genie and Chelsea (as described in Laurence and Margolis (236)) never mastering grammar because of their late exposure, are demonstrative of how the process of language acquisition conforms to “a fairly rigid biologically determined timeframe” (236).

These observations prove difficult for Tomasello’s case of language acquisition. If linguistic mastery is gathered by use, why doesn’t Chelsea’s (for example) exposure to and use of language lead to her constructing sophisticated grammatical rules? If there were a parallel “critical period” for the faculties of intention reading and pattern finding, proponents should be able to demonstrate some other cognitive skills that are similarly time sensitive.

On the other hand, the case of second language acquisition might be more consistent with a usage-based account. From a usage-based perspective, it might be harder for adults to acquire a second language because learning a second language without using it to communicate something is removing the semantic element of language acquisition from the process. Studies on immigrants to the U.S. argue that second language acquisition correlates better with factors like “length of residence in the new country” (Cowie 2017). In this scenario, second language learners might be able to better construct a new grammar through genuine communicative acts in the target language, because they are also able to read the intentions of whoever is speaking to them.

*

Regardless of which side of this heated debate rife with polemic, partisanship, and politics philosophers find themselves on, they must concede that the idea of innateness, fuelled by a POS argument, has changed how scientists and philosophers alike approach linguistics and cognitive science.

Through an exploration of the POS argument, it’s clear that there is some merit to the nativist cause. A strict behaviourist account of language acquisition (closest to Laurence and Margolis’ empiricist), for example, seems utterly incapable of accounting for the complexity of speech based only on linguistic inputs: it seems impossible that an empiricist learner of this kind could acquire language.

Tomasello’s proposal of a usage-based theory of language acquisition, however, presents a compelling counter-argument It claims the difference between input and output does not warrant a language acquisition device. In this view, a child has access to an array of non-linguistic data when constructing grammar in addition to the nativist’s exclusively linguistic input, increasing the quality and quantity of input she has for the language acquisition process. This non-linguistic input takes the form of intentions and patterns, which help her understand the meaning behind communicative acts, and extrapolate individual utterances into a set of syntactic rules respectively. Through the use of language to express her intentions, the learner implicitly recognising patterns and builds syntactic structures to better communicate; she does not learn formal grammatical rules before communicating her intentions about things, and as such mastering language is not as complicated as it seems.

Because semantics and syntax are so intertwined in this view, there is no linking problem, which is a problem nativists have yet to satisfactorily solve. As an innate theory of language acquisition necessarily separates between syntax acquisition and semantic understanding, it must account for how the two are linked for its application of innate syntactic rules to be applicable to particular phonetic utterances.

A comparison of the two approaches to language acquisition shows that the usage-based theory is consistent with what is arguably the core argument in the nativist schema, whereas its application to what seems to be a ‘critical period’ in the acquisition of language, and other time-sensitive aspects of the process needs development. Given the divergence of theoretical ideas on the matter of language innateness, these matters warrant empirical exploration, which will serve to make the field less speculative and hopefully will shed light on how humans learn to speak.
